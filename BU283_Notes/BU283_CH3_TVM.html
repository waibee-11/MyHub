      • Simple interest is the interest earned only on PV

  • 3.2 - Future Value of a Sum
    • Compound Interest
      • Compound Interest is basically interest earned over interest
      • For example, you put money in a bank. After a term (year / quarter), the bank adds certain interest to your account. This interest is calculated based on your initial account balance. Now, when the next term ends, the bank will once again deposit some more interest into your account. However, this time, the interest will be calculated on the account balance resulting after the bank deposited interest for the first time, and not the initial account balance. This is called compounding.

    • Compounding Rules of Thumb
      • Future Balance increases if rate or period increases
      • Compound interest theory applies to any growth
        • We can use the same formula / theory to calculate Future Sales if sales grow at a constant rate
      • Any length of period can be used
        • The formula can be used with any periods like days, weeks, months, quarters, etc.
        • However, the interest must be matched with the time period
        • For example, if ‘i’ is 12%, it is the annual rate. If interest is being calculated on a monthly basis, you must divide ‘i’ by 12, i.e., your new rate would be 1% per month.
    • Future values of mixed cash flows
      • Suppose you expect to receive multiple cash flows over time, which will be different from one another.
      • To know the combined future value for the cash flows, you have calculate the future value for each one seperately and add them up. This is because, you time period will be different for each cash flow
    • Future value with non-annual compounding
      • This is similar to monthly compounding example
      • If your compounding period is different from a year, you have to update i and n.
        • Let ‘m’ be the number of compoundings per year. You divide ‘i’ by ‘m’, and multiply ‘n’ by ‘m’.
        • For example, if the compounding is monthly, ‘m’ would be 12, since there will be 12 compoundings in 1 year [12 months in a year]
        • So, you have multiply ‘n’ by 12, and divide ‘i’ by 12.
