  • Future Value of Streams of Payments
    • Introduction
      • Often, we may need to calculate the future value of a series of equal deposits over a period.
      • You can do this by computing the future value of every single payment, however, there is a better way to do it
    • Annuity
      • Annuity is a series of equal payments made at equal intervals.
      • They don’t have to be made annually. It could be semi-annually, quarterly, monthly, weekly, or even daily.
      • Ordinary annuity is a series of payments that are made at the end of each period.
      • Annuity due is a series of payments that are made at the start of each period.
      • NOTE: UNLESS SPECIFIED OTHERWISE, ALWAYS ASSUME ORDINARY ANNUITIES

  • Tips for Solving Complex TVM Problems
    • General Tips
      • Review the information given in the problem and jot down what is known.
      • Draw a timeline [trust me, it may feel weird, but a timeline helps a lot]
      • Select a focal date [i.e. choose a date to bring all the amounts to using PV and FV formulae]
      • Determine whether the cash flows are lump sums or annuities. Determine if annuities are perpetual.
      • Determine the compounding frequency of the problem (e.g., annual, monthly, or weekly) and decide whether the cash flows are beginning-of-period or end-of-period.
      • PRACTICE, PRACTICE, AND PRACTICE
    • Imbedded Annuities
      • When an annuity is mixed with other irregular payments, we have an imbedded annuity.
      • This is said to occur when annuity payments begin after a few periods [for example, annuity payments may begin after the third year].
      • In such cases, we first find the present value of the annuity and then add on other present values to it.
  • Balloon Loans
    • A balloon loan is where the principal and interest are paid at the end of the term. The end-of-term payment is often referred to as the balloon payment.
  • Amortized Loans
    • Amortized Loan Payments
      • Interest on amortized loan payments comes in two forms
        • each payment contains interest
        • the lender receives the payments before the end of the term and so can earn interest by investing them.
      • The future value of the amortized loan payments is only equal to the balloon payment if the lender can reinvest intermediate payments at the loan rate.
      • Thus, lenders only earn i% on amortized loans if they can reinvest at i%. This is called reinvestment rate assumption
