A1 The Basics of Organizational Behaviour Research

  • A hypothesis is a formal statement of the expected relationship between two variables.
    • Variables are simply measures that can take on two or more values.
      • The less fair (independent variable) people perceive their pay to be, the more likely they will be to quit (dependent variable) their jobs.
  • Independent variable is a predictor or cause of variation in a dependent variable.
    • Dependent variable is a variable that will vary as a result of changes in the independent variable.
  • Moderating variable is a variable that affects the nature of the relationship between an independent and a dependent variable such that the relationship depends on the level of the moderating variable.
    • In the example about attendance bonus, a moderating variable might be pay satisfaction. If the attendance bonus only reduces the absenteeism of employees who are not satisfied with their pay and has no effect on the absenteeism of employees who are satisfied with their pay, then we would conclude that pay satisfaction moderates the effect of the bonus on absenteeism.
  • A mediating variable is a variable that intervenes or explains the relationship between an independent and a dependent variable.
    • We might want to know why bonus pay reduces absenteeism.
      • One possibility is that the bonus increases workers‚Äô perceived fairness.
        • Thus, fairness perceptions intervene or mediate the relationship between the attendance bonus and absenteeism.

Measurement of Variables

  • Reliability is an index of consistency of a research subject‚Äôs responses.
    • If we ask someone several questions about how fair their pay is, their responses should be roughly the same to each question.
  • Validity is an index of the extent to which a measure truly reflects what it is supposed to measure.
    • A good measure of perceived pay fairness should not be influenced by employees‚Äô feelings or fairness about other workplace factors, such as supervision.
      • Convergent validity is when there is a strong relationship between different mesures of the same variable.
        • A measure of job satisfaction should be highly correlated to other measures of job satisfaction.
      • Discriminant validity is when there is a weak relationship between measures of different variables.
        • A measure of job satisfaction should not be strongly related to measures of job performance.

A2 Observational Techniques

  • Observational research examines the natural activities of people in an organizational setting by listening to what they say and watching what they do.

Participant Observation

  • Observational research in which the researcher becomes a functioning member of the organizational unit being studied.
    • Offers a more personal experience than simply interviewing others.
    • Potential for secrecy, subjects should not know that they are being observed.

Direct Observation

  • Observational research in which the researcher observes organizational behaviour without taking part in the studied activity. Why opt for direct over participant?
    • There are many situations in which the introduction of a new person into an existing work setting would severely disrupt and change the nature of the activities in that setting.
    • There are many job tasks that a trained behavioural scientist could not be expected to learn for research purposes.
      • For instance, placing the researcher in a hospital setting where they would be studying and replicating surgeons. Or in an airfield studying pilots.
    • Participant observation also places limitations on being able to record information since you need to be engaged in the workplace you chose to study.
  • An example of direct observation includes:
    • Watching managers work at their desks.
    • Attending meetings with them.
    • Listening to their phone calls.
    • Inspecting their mail.
  • Both observation techniques have weaknesses.
    • There are behaviours that are hard to grasp from a manager such as how they would react to unforeseen circumstances.
    • Limited observers/researchers make it hard to reveal unbiased findings or perceptions. Similarly, too many researchers would drastically change the climate or culture of the company as people would become aware.
  • Observational techniques should be used to allow for an initial examination of some organizational event on which little information is available and to generate ideas for further investigation with more refined techniques.

A3 Correlational Techniques

  • Correlational research attempts to measure variables precisely and examine relationships among these variables without introducing change into the research setting.
    • Correlational research sacrifices some of the depth and richness of the observational techniques for more precision and greater control.
      • Since observational techniques generally involve a single individual listing their findings, little can be said about reliability and validity of the data.
  • Correlational studies involves surveys and interviews as well as existing data.
    • Surveys involve the use of questionnaires to gather data from participants.
    • Interviewing is a technique in which the researcher asks respondents a series of questions to gather data on the variables of interest.
    • Existing data is obtained from organizational records and can include productivity, absence, turnover, and demographic information.
      • An example of a survey question is the extent to which employees are satisfied with their jobs.
  • Evidence of the validity of a questionnaire might come from its ability to predict which employees would quit the organization for work elsewhere.
  • Evidence of the reliability comes from the fact that the responses provided in the surveys are fairly stable.
    • An example hypothesis is as follows: older employees will be absent less than younger employees; to test this a researcher might obtain data concerning the age of employees and their absenteeism from organizational records.
  • Cross-sectional design is the independent and dependant variables that are measured at the same time.
  • Longitudinal design is the independent variable measured at one point in time and the dependant variable measured at some other time.
    • Longitudinal designs are essential in situations where the independent variable precedes the dependant variable.
      • It's also useful for studies that tend to take a longer course of time such as studying how newcomers adjust to socialization structures in the company. (Not something that could be determined quickly)

Correlation and Causation

  • Consider a hypothesis that: ‚Äúfriendly, considerate supervisors will have more productive employees than unfriendly, inconsiderate supervisors‚Äù. In this case a researcher might have some employees describe the friendliness of the supervisors on a reliable, valid questionnaire designed to measure this table and obtain employees productivity levels from company records.
    • In general it would appear that the hypothesis is confirmed.
      • However the answer is no. Should an organization attempt to select friendly supervisors or even train existing supervisors to be friendlier to obtain higher productivity?
        • The training and selection proposal assumes that friendly supervisors cause their employees to be productive but this might not be the case. Put simply, supervisors might be friendly if their employees are productive.

A4 Experimental Techniques

  • Experimental research is research that changes or manipulates a variable under controlled conditions and examines the consequences of this manipulation for some other variable.
    • Let's use our previous hypothesis that: ‚Äúfriendly considerate supervisors will have more productive employees than unfriendly inconsiderate supervisors‚Äù.
      • This means that the style of supervision becomes the independent variable, and productivity becomes a dependant variable.
        • To make this happen, we would need to have supervisors engage in human relations training designed to teach them to be more considerate and personable towards their workers.

        The line on the graph represents the average productivity of a number of employees whose supervisors have received the training. Received the productivity increase and remained higher following the introduction of the training.

        • We cannot be sure that something else did not occur at the time of the training to influence productivity, such as a change in equipment or an employee recognition program.
          • This is where we introduce a control group. A control group is a group of research participants who have not been exposed to the experimental treatment.
            • In this situation, the control group would be the supervisors who have not been exposed to the training.

        In this exhibit, we see that the productivity of the employees whose supervisor were trained increases following training, while that of the control supervisors remains constant. We can then infer that the human relations training did in fact affect employee productivity.

        • Another important characteristic of experimental research is random assignment of participants to experimental and control conditions.
        • Poor sampling due to a lack of random assignment would bias the results in favour of/or against our hypothesis. To achieve random assignment, it would be a good idea to ascertain that the employees of the experimental and control supervisors were equally productive before the training even began.
      • Random assignment is a good way to ensure internal validity, which refers to the extent to which a researcher can be confident that changes in a dependant variable are due to the independent variable.
        • Similarly, factors that are alternative explanations for the results of an experiment are called threats to internal validity. Threats to internal validity are common when there is no control group.

      Threats to internal validity.

      • When the participants are not randomly assigned to the experimental and control conditions, we refer to the study technique as a quasi-experimental design.

A5 A Continuum of Research Techniques

  • All of the research techniques just studied; these methods can be placed on a continuum ranging from rich, broad-based, and loosely controlled to specific, precise, and rigorous experimentation.

Combining Research Techniques

  • Although we have described each of these techniques as independent approaches for studying organizational behaviour, very often they are combined and used to complement each other.
    • Techniques can be used in combination or in series to complement each other.
      • A correlational study followed by an experiment is an example of this. This also goes to show that correlation does not equal causation.

A6 Issues and Concerns in Organizational Behaviour Research

  • Three of these issues include sampling, Hawthorne effects, and ethical concerns.

Sampling

  • The extent to which the results of a study generalized to other samples and settings is known as external validity.
    • External validity will be greater when the results of a study are based on large, random samples.
      • Large samples ensured that the results that are obtained are truly representative of the individuals, groups, or organizations being studied are not merely the product of an extreme case or two.
  • Random samples ensured that all relevant individuals have an equal probability of being studied and give confidence in the generalizability of the findings, however, generalizing from such studies can be problematic.
    • A good way to ensure that random sampling works, is to develop well-designed observational studies that answer specific important questions.

Hawthorne Effect

  • The Hawthorne effect refers to a favorable response of participants in an organizational experiment to a factor other than the independent variable that is formally being manipulated.
    • To return to the human relations training experiment, a Hawthorne effect might occur if the experimental participants are grateful to management for selecting them for this special training and resolve to work harder back on the job.
      • Supervisors might put in longer hours thinking of ways to improve productivity that have nothing to do with the training they received.
        • Researchers have concluded that this other factor is psychological in nature.
  • It is very difficult to prevent Hawthorne effects. Though it is possible and expensive to see whether they have occurred.
    • To do so, investigators establish a second experimental group that receives special treatment and attention but is not exposed to the key independent variable.
  • In the human relations experiment, this could involve training that is not expected to increase employee productivity. If the productivity of the supervisors employees in both experimental groups increase is equally, the author and effect is probably present.
    • Likewise if the productivity only increases in the first scenario where the training improves productivity then one can conclude that the Hawthorne effect was not present since one can be confident that it is due to the human relations training that productivity increased.

Ethics

  • Researches and organizational behaviour, no matter who employs them, have an ethical obligation to do rigorous research and to report that research accurately.
    • In all cases, the psychological and physical well-being of the research participants is of prime importance.
      • In general, ethical researchers avoid unnecessary deception, inform participants about the general purpose of the research, and protect the anonymity of research participants.
        • For example, in a correlational study involving the use of questionnaires, researchers should explain the general reason for the research and a Ford potential participants the opportunity to decline participation.
          • If names or company identification numbers are required to match responses with data in employee files, researchers must guarantee that they will not make individual responses public.
  • Cooperation is easier to obtain when people are confident that ethical procedures are a rule and not an exception.
