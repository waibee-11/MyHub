13.4 Managing Conflict with Negotiation

We define negotiation as “a decision-making process among interdependent parties who do not share identical preferences”

  • Negotiation constitutes conflict management in that it is an attempt to either prevent or resolve conflict

It is common to distinguish between distributive and integrative negotiation tactics

  1. Distributive negotiation
    1. Assumes a zero-sum, win-lose situation in which a fixed pie is divided up between the parties
    1. In theory, the parties will more or less tend toward some compromise
  1. Integrative negotiation
    1. Assumes that mutual problem solving can result in a win-win situation in which the pie is actually enlarged before distribution

Distributive and integrative negotiations can take place simultaneously

Distributive Negotiation Tactics

Essentially single-issue negotiation

  • Suppose you find a used car that you really like
    • With the used-car market, for the most part things usually boil down to the price
    • You want to buy this car for the minimum reasonable price, while the seller wants to sell the car for the maximum reasonable price

Threats and Promises (Assume Party is a Consulting Firm, Using the Image Above)

Threat consists of implying that you will punish the other party if this person does not concede with your position

  • The Other firm might imply that it will terminate its other business with the consulting company if Party does not lower its price on the attitude survey job

Promises are pledges that concessions will lead to rewards in the future

  • Other might promise future consulting contracts if Party agrees to do the survey at a lower price

The difference between a threat and a promise can be subtle, as when the promise implies a threat should no concession be made

  • Threats can have some merit as a bargaining tactic if one party has power over the other, especially if no future negotiations are expected or if the threat can be posed in a civil and subtle way

Promises have merit when you side lacks power and anticipates future negotiations with the other side

Firmness Versus Concessions

Intransigence - sticking to your target position, offering few concessions, and waiting for the other party to give in

  • Research shows that such tactic yields superior economic results, especially in face-to-face negotiations

When some concessions are thought to be appropriate, good negotiators often use face-saving techniques (a way to preserve one’s reputation, credibility, or dignity)

  • For example, the consulting firm might claim that it could reduce the cost of the survey by making it web-based rather than paper-based

Persuasion

Verbal persuasion or debate is common in negotiations; usually in the form of a two-pronged attack

  1. One prong asserts the technical merits of the party’s position
    1. “We have the most qualified staff. We do the most reliable surveys”
  1. The other prong asserts the fairness of the target position
    1. “The company would incur $… worth of expenses in conducting the survey”

Verbal persuasion is an attempt to change the attitudes of the other party toward your target position

  • Most effective when you are an expert (of your topic), likeable, and unbiased

The big problem in distributive negotiations is bias

  • Each party knows the other is self-interested
    • Here, the best way to deal with this conflict is to introduce unbiased parties
      • “Here are just a few of our satisfied clients/firms that have provided a testimony for our surveys”

Salary negotiations are an example of a distributive negotiation

Integrative Negotiation Tactics

Integrative strives for a collaborative problem solving that advances the interests of both parties

Copious Information Exchange

Most of the information exchange in distributive bargaining is concerned with attacking the other party’s position and trying to persuade them of the correctness of yours

  • We tend to be a bit paranoid about information being used against us in bargaining situations

A freer flow of informative is crucial towards finding an integrative settlement

  • One way to proceed is to give away some non-critical information to the other party to get the ball rolling
    • Ask the other party a lot of questions, and make sure to provide the same effort in listening to their responses
    • If all goes well, both parties will begin to reveal their true interests, and not just their side of the argument

Framing Differences as Opportunities

Parties in a negotiation often differ in their preferences, for everything from the timing of a deal to the degree of risk that each party wants to assume

  • Differences = Barriers to negotiation
  • However, differences can serve as a basis for integrative agreements because, they also contain information that can signal the other party’s true interests
    • If coworkers are working on a project and one would like to get the project done earlier on, while the other just wants to meet the deadline
    • Here an integrative solution that frames their differences as opportunities would involve the first person working on the first half of the project while the second, the second half

Cutting Costs

If you can somehow cut the costs that the other party associates with an agreement, the chance for an integrative agreement increases

  • Suppose you are negotiating with your boss for a more interesting job assignment, but they do not like the idea as they rely on your skills to excel in your current task
    • If you ask the right questions, you’d figure they are actually more concerned in you being able to do the job properly
  • A solution that involves cutting costs, would involve you telling your boss that you have trained another employee to take over your role while you start the new assignment; this way they have a guarantee that your current task is being managed

Increasing Resources

Two parties working together might have access to twice as many resources as one party

Introducing Superordinate Goals

Superordinate goals are attractive outcomes that can be achieved only be collaboration

  • Over the years, several terrorist attacks have created superordinate goals that prompt collaboration among nations

How to Negotiate

Assess personal goals, consider others’ goals, and develop a strategy

Identify the target and resistance points

  • Target is something you’d want to achieve
  • Resistance is the lowest outcome acceptable
    • Maybe you are interested in negotiating for a $3 raise, though you’d still be content with a $1.50 raise

Identify BATNA

  • Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement
  • A party’s BATNA refers to what a party can fall back on if a negotiation proves unsuccessful

Staking Out the Bargaining Zone

  • Buyer is hopeful to getting their driveway repainted in the $400-$475 range, while the seller believes their services are valued in the $525-$600 range
    • An obvious bargaining zone would be in the gray area of these two ranges where it would be reasonable for both parties

BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)

  1. BATNAs exist when negotiations are not agreeable to the parties involved
  1. A BATNA represents the most attractive option available if negotiations fail
  1. BATNAs are used to determine the reservation value - the worse possible offer a negotiator is willing to accept
  1. Negotiators can also improve their position by exploring multiple BATNAs
  1. If possible, general negotiations are often a better and quicker strategy

An example of BATNA

  • You are interested in selling your car to someone else for $8K, so $8K becomes your BATNA (but ideally you’d also be open to receiving up to $10K)
  • Though if your minimum selling price is $8K, then a deal will not be made if the buyer would offer a max $7K for your car (for the buyer, $7K would become their BATNA - worst possible offer)
    • So for you, the seller, your Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement, if the buyer offers $5.5K, you should consider another buyer instead
    • For the buyer, if the car is offered at $9K, your Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement, would be to take your business elsewhere
      • Though if the buyer offers a price within the ‘bargaining zone’, $7.5K, then both parties should be able to come to an agreement (a bit lower than what you’d value your car, and a bit higher than what the buyer would value your car)

Common Mistakes in Negotiation

  1. Irrational Escalation of Commitment
    1. Continuing a previously chosen course of action beyond what a rational analysis would recommend, this is also called sunk cost fallacy
    1. Purchasing a sub for $10, and choosing to put yourself through the suffering of eating it even if you severely dislike it; you already invested the $10 so you’d be reluctant to abandon it
      1. You are committing yourself to a decision that is not in your best interest; you could instead go back and argue with the worker that made your sub (don’t actually do this)
  1. Belief in the Mythical Fixed-Pie
    1. The assumption that your own interests directly conflict with the other party
      1. You want to ask your IA for a regrade of your assignment, but you are of the belief that they would never do such a thing, or even further lower your mark
        1. Essentially, never make assumptions, you do not know their interests, and they don’t know yours so unless you go up to them and ask, a solution cannot be determined
      1. This is win-lose thinking
  1. Anchoring and Adjustment
    1. Anchor is a standard against which future adjustments are measured
    1. Very often, the choice of an anchor is based on faulty or incomplete information
    1. The best way to solve this problem is to do your research and due diligence
      1. You are applying for a job where one of the questions ask, what is your optimal salary? Well, you’d try to frame your response on the industry standard for salaries of that position so that your response is not unreasonable either for yourself (too less) or the company (too much)

Anchors

  • Base figures from which we add or subtract
  • People constantly look for them in making judgments
  • Can be set by: previous price, industry standard, rumors, bids or offers
  • We are predisposed, in a way to focus on an anchor - even when the anchor has little applicability to the current situation

Frames

  • The type of information brought into the negotiation
  • Word choice and phrasing can influence how elements of a negotiation are interpreted and understood
  • We are more influenced by the psychological pain of loss than the psychological rewards of gain
    • A stock broker tells you that the risk of buying a stock is high with the potential for loss of money, though the potential is also there to receive a high return
    • Generally, we would be much more fixated towards the risk factor, than the gain

Third Party Involvement

Third parties may come into play to intervene between negotiating parties when an impasse or deadlock is reached or they may be involved from the start as a normal part of the bargaining process; there are two approaches to this

  1. Mediation
    1. When a neutral third party helps to facilitate a negotiated agreement by aiding the process or by intervening in the content of the negotiation
  1. Arbitration
    1. When a neutral third party is given the authority to dictate the terms of settlement of a conflict
    1. Usually happens when the negotiation has broken down and the arbitrator has to make a final distributive allocation
      1. A judge may be an example of an arbitrator; they listen to what both sides have to say and decide a meaningful response
    1. There are two types of arbitration
      1. Conventional
        1. Arbitrator can choose any outcome, such as splitting the difference between the parties
      1. Final offer
        1. Each party makes a final offer, and the arbitrator chooses one of them
    1. Arbitration is usually a last resort as there is risk involved in this for both parties; things could go completely south and you’d lose more through arbitration than you would have in the initial negotiation; you also usually can’t negotiate with an arbitrator so all decisions are finalized
